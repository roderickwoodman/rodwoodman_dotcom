Dinsmore Landscape Company

In this website redesign I was allowed to completely overhaul the current design in both look and functionality. I reworked the copy and the information architecture, added my own original photography of the client's properties, and made the site work great on mobile. This project doubled as the final requirement for my USCS Extension Web Design Certificate, so it was a true start-to-finish project for a client that I found myself. I am still getting emails from this client about how happy he is that the new site is generating leads for his business.

Step #1: Planning

The project started out with emails and telephone calls with the client, who is the owner of the business. I determined that the company's brand is one of quality and responsiveness, and that their competitive advantage, besides being local, is that they also maintain fountains on the properties that they service. The goal for the website would be to drive users to contact the company. I collected assets not present on the company website like logos and brochures, and I drafted a requirements document.

Step #2: Design

It was obvious from the inconsistent design of the assets gathered that the client had no brand focus. So my design would include a prominent logo, matching the logo version from their trucks,Â as well as other visuals that communicated quality and responsiveness, like trade association logos, testimonials, and a contact form. It was also necessary to reduce the page count and tighten up the information architecture of the website. With the new site map in mind, I walked the client's job sites and took photos of their work in order to replace the poor-quality, unflattering photos of the existing design. Before the photos were all collected, I met with my client and he approved my design.

Step #3: Implementation

My implementation goal was to start with a simple, functional website, and to enhance as much as possible as time permitted. The basic div structure using HTML5 & CSS3 was straightforward to create from scratch, but once I added the responsive elements via media queries and started testing on mobile devices, single-pixel issues started to consume some time. I got the client's IT company to create a "customerservice" email ID for branding improvement. Adding the web form that would trigger this new email took more time than expected because the hosting company Rackspace required SMTP and not the simple PHP mail() function, and also because I wanted the form submit action to be as seamless of an experience as possible. The other big piece was the continual manipulation of images -- sizing, compression, naming, and placement to ensure consistency and unity with the copy. I then switched my design live after my second meeting with my client, where he approved my implementation. With the luxury of more time I would have enhanced the site further by making the images more lively and clickable.
